Nephrotic syndrome is one of the most common kidney diseases in childhood, characterized by heavy proteinuria, low blood albumin levels, high cholesterol, and edema.

## Main Symptoms

The most obvious symptom is swelling (edema), usually starting around the eyes and gradually spreading to the face, legs, and entire body. Parents may notice puffy eyes in the morning.

Other common symptoms include decreased urine output, foamy urine (a sign of protein in urine), poor appetite, and fatigue.

## Diagnosis

Doctors typically use the following tests:
- Urinalysis to detect protein levels
- 24-hour urine protein quantification
- Blood tests for albumin and cholesterol levels
- Kidney biopsy when necessary

## Treatment

The primary treatment is corticosteroids (such as prednisone). Most children respond well to steroid therapy. Treatment usually takes several months, and parents must follow the doctor's instructions carefully.

For steroid-resistant or frequently relapsing cases, other immunosuppressive medications may be used.

## Daily Care

- Limit salt intake
- Ensure adequate rest
- Prevent infections and maintain good hygiene
- Regular follow-up with urine and blood tests
- Take medications as prescribed

## Prognosis

Most children with nephrotic syndrome have a good prognosis, especially those with minimal change disease. Although relapses may occur, they typically become less frequent with age.
